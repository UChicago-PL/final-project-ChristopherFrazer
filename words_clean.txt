Quota use within the New York City Police Department is a controversial practice with an equally tumultuous past Quotas are formal or informal demands for police officers to perform a certain amount of arrests summonses citations or other activities within a given timeframe Formal quotas have explicit numerical demands for arrest totals while informal quotas promote activity through unwritten means such as financial incentives disciplinary action unfavorable reassignments and the threat of termination OsseiOwusu  p  For decades quota use has underpinned officer malpractice and poor police work In  the Knapp Commission Report on Police Corruption in the NYPD linked pressure to fulfill quotas to officer misconduct in the form of evidence planting and illegal detainment McCarthy  p  Quotas also facilitated New York Citys stopandfrisk policy which was ruled unconstitutional for its unlawful targeting of minorities Goldstein  Recognizing the danger that quotas posed to public safety and officer autonomy the New York State Senate prohibited their use in  Despite this recent lawsuits affidavits interviews and surveys of NYPD officers indicate that the practice is alive and well
This paper will analyze the mechanisms that allow quotas to persist in the modern NYPD focusing on the organizational goals and structural incentives that promote quota use It will first demonstrate how modern quantitative performance metrics along with stringent supervision of officers effectively institutionalize quotas Then it will discuss the negative repercussions of quota use on police officer quality of life and the systemic oppression of socioeconomically disadvantaged minority communities Finally it will provide recommendations to curb the use of quotas within the department 

II Historical Context
	The supervision of police officers has always been a difficult task Patrolmen possess a high degree of discretion and prior to sophisticated modern tracking systems it was near impossible to know an officers exact location or activity Bronstein  p  This combination of autonomy and freedom from oversight allowed officers to slack off on the job by socializing or idling instead of performing substantive police work Quotas in their most basic form are a managerial strategy that aims to combat such officer inactivity by forcing them to make a certain number of arrests summonses or tickets in a short amount of time Bronstein  p  While motivating police officers to do their jobs is an important goal quotas have a myriad of unintended side effects that in conjunction with specific NYPD objectives can negatively impact both officers and communities
The precise character of quota use in the NYPD developed over time to fit changing departmental goals During the s and early s the NYPDs primary goal was to eliminate rampant corruption among its ranks making quota use an undesirable and detrimental practice PERF  McCarthy  This perspective was heavily influenced by the Knapp Commissions investigation into the NYPD which established the clear connection between quota use and corruption in the fields of narcotics gambling and prostitution OsseiOwusu  p  Subsequent investigations affirmed this belief with the work of experts such as former Police Administrator William McCarthy decrying quotas as the most serious of corruption incentives McCarthy  p  In addition to overwhelming external criticism of quotas the NYPD found that their institutional emphasis on quantity rather than quality of arrests hindered the departments ability to combat the heroin trade Burnham  Testimony at an investigative commission revealed that due to strict quota requirements detectives had no time to make good cases which could adequately prevent heroin sales Burnham   
	McCarthy further delves into the degenerative impact of quotabased quantitative success metrics with a discussion of superstars in NYPD culture Superstars were officers who made the most arrests for felony and misdemeanor charges becoming wellrenowned by peers and supervisors in the process McCarthy  p  McCarthy claims that superstars accrued these impressive numbers through corrupt means but because the quality of their arrests was never scrutinized their corruption went unnoticed McCarthy  p  The departments most prized officers having the least integrity facilitated the corruption of newer more impressionable officers causing superstars to corrupt more policemen than the Mafia McCarthy  p  From the s onward the NYPD was well aware of the risks involved with quotabased policing
	However the development of Broken Windows Theory a policing ideology focused on reducing public disorder threatened to reintroduce quantitative police measurement and incentivize quota use Broken Windows Theory is predicated on the idea that signs of public disorderbroken windows public intoxication graffiti vagrancy etccreate an environment that breeds more serious crime Kelling and Wilson  To combat disorder police departments must crack down on these misdemeanor infractions by increasing police activity in atrisk areas Kelling and Wilson  The theory successfully counteracted the common conception of the time that police could only respond to crime not prevent it
Unprecedented levels of violent crime in the early s forced the NYPD to reorient its mission from anticorruption to crime reduction The department soon adopted the Broken Windows ideology stressing the importance of policing petty crimes in order to uphold the aesthetic of safety in neighborhoods the subway system and other public spaces Bratton  p  These efforts proved fruitful as decreases in felony rates accompanied increases in misdemeanor arrests Bratton  p  The inverse relationship between the number of arrests and felony crime rates established during the Broken Windows era would soon become the guiding principle of NYPD operations PERF  p  Many critics argue that underlying socioeconomic changes namely the economic boom of the s were what actually reduced crime rates although the NYPD swore by the efficacy of its new tactics Francis 

III Measurement in Modern Policing The Impact of Compstat
	What truly allowed the NYPD to pursue its new crimereducing objective was the revolution in crime statistics and police tracking brought about by the development of Compstat PERF  p  Compstat a police management system that couples computerized tracking of crime statistics locations and other relevant data with frequent departmental meetings unlocked the NYPDs potential to prevent crime PERF  p vii Prior to Compstat it was near impossible for the department to define successful police work Officers operated in a primarily reactive manner responding to  calls and other crime reports without proactively implementing crimereduction initiatives PERF  p  This lack of a compelling organizational purpose was due in large part to the departments inability to quantify the successes or failures of its activities In the absence of timely police statistics officers could not know if their work which prioritized quick and effective responses to reported crimes made any tangible impact on the safety of communities PERF  p  Compstat fixed this issue by providing a clear framework to evaluate police success in the form of crime statistics If crime rates decreased the NYPD was operating as intended and if not changes had to be made An internal NYPD publication attests to this shift in priorities stating NYPD officers watch weekly crime trends with the hawklike attention private corporations pay to profits and loss PERF  p  Police work in the NYPD quickly became the business of crime reduction with Compstats statistics as its bottom line PERF  p 
	Beyond the reinvention of internal management the NYPDs organizational shift toward Compstat and crime reduction redefined its relationship with two key constituenciespoliticians and the public The reliability and publicity of postCompstat crime statistics allowed politicians to run on effective tough on crime platforms Rudy Gulliani was the first New York City mayoral candidate to fully embrace policing as a means of ensuring public safety a decision that significantly aided his victory in the  mayoral race Santiago  Decreasing crime rates became not only the indicator of NYPD success but also a referendum on governmental competence Moreover crime statistics newfound importance in political life made public perception of safety inexorably tied with crime rate fluctuations CPDC  
These changes were ultimately beneficial for the NYPD Its close relationship with the public and city government allows the department to wield crime statistics as both a sword and a shield If crime rates decrease year after year then departmental initiatives are effective and should be supported both financially and legally by the city Riley  If crime rates are increasing then the department must not have enough resources to operate correctly making increases in budget andor authority necessary in order to get crime under control The impact of such logic was clearly displayed during the  Defund the Police movement which faced fierce backlash due to a simultaneous increase in crime rates Akinnibi  Crime increases scared the population causing public opinion to swing in favor of high police presence within the city Akinnibi  Consequently defending the police became a politically unviable platform as leading mayoral candidates expressed their hesitancy to scale back NYPD operations Akinnibi  The absolute necessity of the NYPD in times of peace and turmoil makes police reform efforts difficult to market allowing the departments current practices to go widely unchallenged
Tethered to the numbers the NYPD would soon have to grapple with the byproducts of its commitment to Compstat This paper will discuss how these byproducts result in the institutionalization of quotas which both strain police culture and oppress marginalized communities in New York City

IV Implementation Analysis  The Institutionalization of Quotas Through Performance Evaluations
	Both internal and external pressures on the NYPD incentivize the use of quotas as a means of satisfying expectations However these topdown institutional incentives carry little weight if police officers who actually implement NYPD policy do not act in accordance with departmental objectives Officers have little incentive to prioritize quantity over quality of arrests as doing so eliminates their discretion as professionals to determine which actions pose a legitimate threat to public safety versus which do not Bronstein  p  Without strict oversight this tension between officer autonomy and NYPD objectives would go unresolved hindering the departments ability to implement crimereduction strategies Quotas solve this oversight issue by allowing supervisors to mandate that officers stay in line with department objectives under the threat of punishment for noncompliance Bronstein  p 
	The NYPD implements these quotas by baking them into the necessary organizational practice of performance evaluations Rayman and Brown  Experts Serdar Gul and Paul OConnell define police performance appraisals as a fourstep process where departments  define what performance entails  measure performance  provide feedback to employees and  forward their evaluations to other organizations that require them Gul and OConnell  Evaluating NYPD performance appraisals under this framework it is clear how quotas have become a key facet of the organization
	As discussed in the previous section the NYPD defines success quantitatively using crime statistics PERF  p  Desired reductions in crime rates are best achieved through the strict policing of misdemeanor crimes which is quantified by the number of misdemeanor arrests performed in a given amount of time Bratton  p  The success of officers who are responsible for the enforcement of citations arrests and summonses is therefore measured by the number of enforcement activities they perform during their shifts Supervisors then provide feedback based on officers measured activity Carver  If officers have a high number of arrests summonses and citations they are praised If not they are reprimanded If this incentive structure sounds familiar its because this quantitative evaluation of officer success is precisely what underpinned the use of quotas in the s Burnham  The only difference between these two eras is that the incentive for quota use originates not from the individual inclination of supervisors but from topdown pressure to produce good numbers imposed by the NYPD itself Bronstein  p  The final step in appraisalthe forwarding of evaluations to other superiorsalso reinforces quota use because supervisors are viewed as incompetent if their officers do not perform up to departmental standards Bronstein  p 
	Over the past decade numerous lawsuits secret recordings and testimonials have exposed the exact methods by which supervisors impose quotas on officers in spite of their illegality All three forms of evidence for quota use can be found in the proceedings of Floyd v City of New York the groundbreaking stopandfrisk lawsuit that claimed that police unlawfully stopped men of color Carver  Officer Adhyl Polanco testified that during a daily roll call meeting supervisors told him to perform a minimum of five stopandfrisks  tickets and one arrest per month Carver  This was a demand not a suggestion as indicated by his supervisors threat that Polanco do it or you are going to become a Pizza Hut deliveryman Caver  Another NYPD officer Pedro Serrano taped daily roll calls recording supervisors demanding more stopandfrisks from officers Carver  This statement was similarly reinforced by a threat with supervisors stating afterward If you dont want to be on a foot post get a collar an arrest Carver  Deputy Chief Michael Marino justified these practices arguing that department managers can and must set performance goals in order to combat officer laziness and underperformance Carver  These performance goals are completely indistinguishable from quotas however as they either resemble formal quotas through the statements Officers must perform X number of activities in Y amount of time or mirror informal quotas by pressuring officers to boost their activity numbers Rayman and Brown 
	In addition supervisors utilize comparative performance evaluations to drive up arrest numbers Whistleblower Craig Matthews exposed how supervisors in the Bronxs nd Precinct made officers compete to determine who could perform the most arrests summonses and stopandfrisks Mathias  Official police documents then categorized officers into groups that met exceeded or underperformed supervisor standards Mathias  The pittance of officers against each other increased pressure to perform arrests regardless of their quality or legality Mathias  Moreover officer competition negatively impacted police culture an issue that this paper will soon discuss in more detail 
	Supervisors coerce officers into complying with quotas through the manipulation of financial incentives employment opportunities and quality of life decisions Numerous officers across multiple lawsuits have affirmed through affidavits and testimony that the NYPD deprives officers who fail to meet quotas of overtime pay Rayman and Brown  Furthermore negative appraisals awarded to quota noncompliant officers preclude them from career advancement opportunities such as promotions Gul and OConnell  Finally undesirable assignments were given to poorly performing officers Carver  The fear of punishment through these avenues compels officers to comply with quotas even if the practice contradicts their personal ideological values Rayman and Brown  
	Supervisors are incentivized to enforce quotas for similar reasons as quantitative performance evaluations are used at all levels of the NYPD hierarchy Since NYPD higherups are equally obsessed with numbers as officer supervisors they conduct performance appraisals of their inferiors using the same standards Consequently all ranks of NYPD employees seek to extract the bestlooking numbers from those they supervise as a means of upward career mobility Bronstein  p  As one retired detective puts it The Lieutenant wants numbers because he wants to look good at the platoon meeting The Sergeant wants numbers because he wants to look good he wants a better assignment in the command Bronstein  p  Quantitatively grounded objectives and methods of performance appraisal originate from the top and trickle down the NYPD bureaucracy making quota use at the officersupervisor level inevitable
	The aforementioned internal and external pressures for quota use make antiquota legislation unable to curb their implementation in the NYPD Bronstein  p  Despite being banned in  a  survey of  New York Patrolmens Benevolent Association members found that  of officers believed that supervisors imposed quotas McLaughlin  With such a large majority of officers attesting to the use of quotas it is undeniable that they are a fundamental aspect of contemporary NYPD police work The rest of this paper will analyze the impact of quota use on both officers and communities

V Implementation Analysis  Quotas Degenerative Impact on Police Culture
	To understand the ramifications of quota use on police officers themselves we must view them as streetlevel bureaucrats SLBs SLBs first defined by Michael Lipsky in  are officials who make policy through meaningful interactions with citizens Lipsky  p  SLBs are characterized by high degrees of discretion in determining the nature amount and quality of benefits and sanctions as well as relative autonomy from organizational authority Lipsky  p  Police officers are quintessential streetlevel bureaucrats because they enforce legislation through interactions with citizens wield a significant amount of discretion in this enforcement and have general autonomy from supervision during patrols This autonomy is often a source of conflict however since SLBs have the authority to act in opposition to organizational goals if they have different ideological dispositions than organizational higherups Lipsky  p  Such differences are primarily due to the antagonistic interests of SLBs and managers stemming from qualitative differences in the requirements of their positions Lipsky  p  Managers work primarily to advance organizational goals while SLBs work according to their individual preferences and ideals complying with organizational policies only when sanctions for not doing so are severe Lipsky  p  An inherently adversarial interest among all SLBs is the expansion and maintenance of their autonomy against managerial restrictions Lipsky  p  
	In their daytoday activities police officers exercise an incredible amount of discretion at every level of enforcement While patrolling officers witness far more infractions than they can possibly police It is thus incumbent upon them to decide which infractions are serious enough to respond to or insignificant enough to ignore Bronstein  p  Once an officer decides to respond to unlawful activity they can handle the interaction in a multitude of ways They can arrest the individual for the crime committed arrest the individual and then charge them with a less serious crime or give them a verbal warning Bronstein  p  Broad officer discretion is constitutive of their professionalism and is a tool they use to forge positive relationships with the communities they serve Bronstein  p  Nevertheless officer discretion does not necessarily align with NYPD goals In instances where supervisors view discretion as an impediment to productive police work they will implement regulations that force officers to take departmentsanctioned enforcement action in previously discretionary scenarios Bronstein  p  The triedandtested method of regulation favored by the NYPD is the use of quotas
	The NYPDs use of quotas exacerbates the latent officersupervisor antagonism by eliminating numerous facets of officer discretion Quotas tell officers who to arrest where to patrol and if an infraction is worthy of enforcement Affidavits from a  lawsuit against the NYPD show that supervisors explicitly told officers to arrest black and Hispanic individuals Rayman and Brown  The four officers involved in the case claim that hard targets were set for black and Hispanic individuals while white Asian and Jewish individuals were given soft targets meaning they should not be arrested for the sake of meeting quota goals Rayman and Brown  Recordings from Floyd v City of New York captured the supervisor of Officer Pedro Serrano identifying male blacks age  to  as the perpetrators of robberies and grand larcenies in the south Bronx corroborating these claims of racialized quota enforcement Carver  With the target demographics for quota use defined officers are sent to minority neighborhoods to combat crime Carver  Once there officers crack down on the same misdemeanor offenses that they used to ignore out of necessity to fulfill lofty quotas for arrests citations and summonses Bronstein  p 
	Without discretionary capabilities officers lose their sense of professionalism putting their personal values and job satisfaction at odds with quotaimposed requirements This internal dissonance is especially pronounced for officers of color who have experienced the racial aspect of quota enforcement firsthand Officer Serrano cited his experience being Hispanic in the Bronx where he was frequently stopped for arbitrary reasons as a reason why he refused to comply with quota demands Carver  In the words of attorney John Scola this toxic culture which permeates the NYPD forces officers to choose between standing up for what is right and being able to feed their families Rayman and Brown  Recognizing the danger of such a culture the New York State Senate cited the elimination of police professionalism as a justification for its legislation banning the use of quotas NYSenate  
	Furthermore the NYPDs insular culture led to the ostracization of offices that spoke out against quota use Internal secrecy and loyalty to the department have long been staples of the NYPD making policedriven reform efforts incredibly difficult The most damning cause of this culture is the lack of organizational avenues to report wrongdoing corruption or illegal activity within the department McCarthy  p  In the s officers who attempted to expose corruption were labeled snitches and stool pigeons by their peers McCarthy  p  The contemporary culture is no different as officers consciously suppress their moral outrage over the use of quotas due to fear of social ostracization and ruined employment prospects The story of Edwin Raymond an officer who openly opposed the use of quotas displays NYPD cultures systemic repression of dissent Raymond a model officer with outstanding credentials and past evaluations received scathing performance evaluations due to his low numbers of arrests even though crime rates in the areas he patrolled were declining Knafo  In private conversations Raymonds direct supervisor Martin Campbell expressed frustration over these evaluations since pressure came from his superior to deduct Raymond for low arrest numbers Knafo  While ideologically opposed to these quotabased evaluations Campbell refused to submit a letter of recommendation for Raymond fearing that doing so would reflect poorly on himself and jeopardize his chances at upward mobility in the department Knafo  When confronted by Raymond about this decision Campbell said I have to protect myself my job and my family Knafo  The NYPDs culture of fear reproduces the power dynamic between officer and supervisor at all levels of departmental hierarchy making opposition to quota use an untenable position
	Beyond employmentrelated retribution for whistleblowing officers fear physical and psychological harassment for speaking out After Officer Serrano voiced his concerns about being docked overtime pay for quota noncompliance to Internal Affairs fellow officers defaced his locker with stickers of rats Carver  The message was clear comply with department practices or face humiliation Officer Adrian Schoolcraft endured even more appalling retaliation for reporting supervisor abuses to NYPD higherups When word reached the implicated supervisors they declared Schoolcrafts actions coupled with his failure to return phone calls a psychiatric emergency Dwyer  A team of officers then raided Schoolcrafts apartment detaining him then transferring him to a psychiatric hospital Dwyer  Schoolcrafts hospitalization lasted six days three of which he spent in a locked ward without access to technology Dwyer  For his troubles Schoolcraft received a  medical billa final slap in the face for his attempt to encourage supervisor integrity Dwyer  Schoolcrafts detainment served as a warning to other potential whistleblowers that extreme measures may be taken to shut them up Dwyer  While underhanded these tactics are effective in constructing the blue wall of silence which shields NYPD infractions namely the illegal use of quotas from outside scrutiny and floods of litigation McDonald 

VI Implementation Analysis  The Victims of Quota Use 
	Quotas are an inherently racialized policing tactic Both quantitative and qualitative data validate this claim as numerous officers submitted sworn statements claiming that the NYPD uses racial profiling while crime statistics show a clear racial disparity in arrest numbers Wallace  Departmental encouragement of police presence in minority communities helps account for this and the line from discriminatory police work to racially biased criminal justice outcomes becomes apparent Even after stopandfrisk was deemed unconstitutional for its blatant racial profiling the proportions of arrests by race remained byandlarge unchanged Goldstein  Akinnibi  Despite crime rates decreasing overall in recent years black and Hispanic New Yorkers continue to be arrested at disproportionately high rates Akinnibi  In  alone between  and  of individuals arrested for misdemeanor crimes were black or Hispanic USCCR  p 
	A deeper look into socioeconomic conditions and quota incentives reveals why black and Hispanic communities are targeted New York has a staggering racial income disparity with the average annual income for a white household being  higher than the average black household and  higher than the average Hispanic household FPI  p  Black and Hispanic New Yorkers are also more highly concentrated in lowincome communities as  of black families and  of Hispanic families lie in the bottom half of the income distribution FPI  p  As previously discussed the quality of quota arrests is generally poor with their grounds often lying in officer arrest requirements rather than grievous infractions on the part of the detained Consequently quotaproduced arrests citations and summonses are more likely to be overturned if challenged in court Surico  To avoid this the NYPD puts the brunt of its quota efforts into arresting black and Hispanic individuals in lowincome areas because they lack the funding for legal representation to contest charges in court Surico  Former NYPD officer Anthony Miranda puts it quite plainly Those are your three targeted areas black and Hispanic because they dont have lawyers The chances of fighting back are very little Surico 
	But just how low are these chances False conviction statistics are telling indicators of this since they are caused by certain quotarelated factors including police misconduct eyewitness misidentification and false confessions KWIP For nonwhitecollar felony crimes the breadandbutter of arrests the racial disparity in false conviction rates is staggering False conviction rates of black Americans are  times higher for murder  times higher for sexual assault and  times higher overall than those of white Americans Gross et al  p iiiiv The false imprisonment of minorities for drug crimes best showcases the impact of quotabased racial profiling Because drug crimes are rarely reported to the police officers themselves must seek out and stop perpetrators of these offenses Gross et al  p iv Since quotas are enforced primarily in black and Hispanic communities the majority of individuals arrested for drug offenses are black or Hispanic Carver  These minority detainees unable to afford adequate legal representation and disadvantaged by implicit biases in the judicial system are falsely convicted of drug offenses at a rate nineteen times higher than whites Gross et al  p iv Furthermore  of instances where police fabricated evidence in drug arrests involved black suspects Gross et al  p iv These statistics lay bare the insidious human implications of quota use In the pursuit of goodlooking numbers this practice ruins the lives of countless black and Hispanic New Yorkers For these communities the NYPD frequently acts not as a protective force but as an oppressive one 
	Furthermore individuals in overpoliced socioeconomically disadvantaged communities are more likely to plead guilty to crimes they did not commit This is primarily due to the steep financial costs of legal defense and incredibly lengthy wait times for trial which most victims of quotas spend in custody because they cannot afford to pay bail USCCR  p  Consequently officers can fulfill quotas through shoddy meritless arrests with general impunity since the individuals they target lack the financial or institutional power to fight back Columbia Law School Professor Jefferey Fagan characterizes the impact of these guilty pleas not limited to the ineligibility of convicted citizens for public housing as a racial tax incurred by communities of color USCCR  p   
	The racial tax hinders upward mobility and economic betterment in heavily policed communities Its impact is twofold working individuals are forced to take time off of their jobs to serve punishments for summonses and arrests while individuals charged with crimes have trouble finding jobs after their sentences end Since the targets of quotas are byandlarge near or under the poverty line they cannot pay the fines for summonses or citations forcing them to do community service hours instead USCCR  p  Community service necessitates time off of work worsening the already dire financial situations of individuals in socioeconomically disadvantaged communities USCCR  p  Those who serve jail time are barred from employment opportunities due to their criminal record restricting their access to legitimate means of financial sustenance Knafo  Thus they resort to crime to provide for themselves restarting the cycle of criminalization and fortifying the crime statistics that police use to justify their presence in minority communities USCCR  p 
	Beyond financial and legal detriments to communities of color quotas enact a psychological toll on minority youth A  survey of New York City people of color aged  revealed that most respondents reported fearing involvement with the criminal justice system every time they stepped foot outside their apartment USCCR  p  Police are stationed everywhere in these lowincome minority communitieson street corners in schools in community courtyards etc USCCR  p  Past traumatic experiences watching the brutal arrests of their parents condition youth to fear law enforcement contributing to a sense of alienation and insecurity being in their own neighborhoods USCCR  p  Quotadriven police aggression in minority communities turns these neighborhoods into symbolic and actual jails where the everyday experience of just living becomes criminalized according to John Jay College of Criminal Justice professor Brett Stoudt USCCR  p 
	Finally quotas increase the amount of police brutality and misconduct directed toward communities of color There is always an inherent risk of violence or brutality in police encounters Quotas by increasing the number of police enforcement encounters as well as creating a relationship of distrust between officers and those they intend to serve significantly increase the probability of police brutality PROP  p  Many highprofile and unseen instances of police brutality or excessive use of force stem from seemingly simple arrests for misdemeanor crimes For example the interaction that resulted in the death of Eric Garner at the hands of an NYPD officer occurred because Garner was allegedly selling loose cigarettes PROP  p  To prevent the physical financial and psychological damage inflicted by racially biased quota enforcement sweeping departmental reform is necessary

VII Policy Recommendations
Abandon the Broken Windows Ideology and Quantitative Performance Metrics
To meaningfully combat quota use the policing ideology that incentivizes it must be rejected The efficacy of Broken Windows policing in the modern day is ambiguous at best but its negative impact on the lives of New Yorkers is undeniable A  NYC Office of the Inspector General OIG report indicated that decreases in orderoriented enforcement of misdemeanors and summonses produced no increases in felony crime USCCR  p  This clearly refutes the fundamental presupposition in Broken Windows Theory which is that the maintenance of order in communities via heavy enforcement of minor infractions will lead to reductions in serious crime Moreover there is a lower limit to crime rate reduction and the city is near that breaking point Sparrow  p  As a result aggressive enforcement under Broken Windows is largely redundantachieving little of its intended crimereducing goals while still incurring the negatives of worsened policecommunity relationships toxic police culture and the systemic oppression of lowincome minority communities Sparrow  p 
In the absence of an ideological shift antiquota reform through legislation or internal department initiatives carries little weight As previously discussed state prohibitions were incredibly ineffective at curbing quota use because the NYPD had no incentive to change its practices and could evade litigation by encoding quotas in productivity goals and other euphemisms The fluid nature of informal police quotas will allow the practice to continue as long as the department deems them valuable a mindset inherent to Broken Windows policing 
	The discontinuance of quantitative performance evaluations is the second step in quota deinstitutionalization The pressure that these success standards place on NYPD personnel across the departments hierarchy ties quota use into occupational wellbeing A more holistic approach to performance appraisals would eliminate these incentives and by extension quota use Some qualitative standards of success that address public safety concerns while encouraging departmental efficiency are community relationships integrity innovation officer satisfaction quality of life improvements and administrative effectiveness Sparrow  
Increase Accountability Through Departmental Reform and Civilian Oversight
	A key factor in combating misconduct is knowing when it occurs Officers are the first and often only individuals to hear quotas being implemented in realtime at daily roll call meetings and in conversations with their supervisors Carver  Current departmental culture suppresses the flow of this information however as officers who come forward with reports of misconduct face verbal psychological and physical harassment as well as a decreased likelihood of promotion Hence providing officers with secure and discrete avenues to report experienced misconduct will be crucial in the enforcement of preexisting antiquota legislation and directives
Understanding misconduct from a civilian standpoint is just as critical to the elimination of quota use Civilian oversight boards are a triedandtested method of accruing civilian complaints about police misconduct and providing disciplinary recommendations to police departments Finn  p iii The benefits of civilian oversight are well established Communities feel validation when departments implement recommendations and respond to allegations made by the board empowering citizens to hold the police accountable Finn  p xi These expressions of goodwill mend policecommunity relationships while improving the quality of internal investigations into misconduct Finn  p x Such benefits only come when departments value the reports of civilian oversight boards however and inverse effects can occur when communities feel ignored Cramer  Such is the case in New York City where the NYPD in recent years has taken action on far fewer civilian oversight board allegations than before Cramer  To remedy this the department must forge stronger connections with civilian oversight boards and commit to seriously considering all allegations that originate from them

VIII Conclusion
	Conceived as an innocuous police management strategy quotas quickly developed into the NYPDs staple instrument of crime reduction and a parasite that facilitated the systemic oppression of lowincome minority communities Deeply connected these two policy outcomes are the byproduct of shifts in departmental ideology advancements in crime tracking technology and performance evaluations that ensure officer compliance
	The NYPDs adoption of Broken Windows theory first transformed quota use from a decentralized tactic for promoting officer productivity to an institutionwide practice for achieving success The theory presented an intuitive optimistic outlook on the potential of police work to not only respond to but also prevent crime According to the theory reducing felony crime was as simple as restoring the aesthetic of order to atrisk communities by aggressively enforcing misdemeanor offenses Tasked with combating the historic violent crime rates of the s the Department embraced Broken Windows and set its sights on crime rate reduction
	The crime statistics revolution caused by the development of Compstat facilitated the NYPDs new goal Officerspecific crime statistics allowed supervisors to hold their patrolmen accountable for low activity allowing them to set and enforce strict quota goals Supervisors enforced these quotas by threatening noncompliant officers with decreased overtime pay worsened assignments poor evaluations and termination from the department NYPD culture reinforced quota use as officers who reported it faced significant backlash from peers and higherups alike To fulfill these quotas officers descended upon socioeconomically disadvantaged minority communities that lacked the finances or free time to fight meritless charges
	While difficult I believe that with the proper reforms NYPD quota use can be eradicated It will entail a complete overhaul of departmental success standards and evaluation methods Quantitative evaluations must be left in the past replaced by multifaceted performance appraisals that measure integrity community relationships quality of life and other qualitative elements of police work that respect the dignity and wellbeing of all New Yorkers To enforce these reforms and prevent a relapse to old quotabased practices NYPD Internal Affairs must be revamped to provide security and safety to whistleblower officers Moreover community voices must be listened to and the recommendations of civilian oversight boards taken seriously These recommendations will begin the healing process for broken policecommunity relationships while protecting the most vulnerable New Yorkers from quotadriven exploitation
